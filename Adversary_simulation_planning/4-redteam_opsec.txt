Part B - Pre-Engagement OpSec Checklist:

[Category 1 - Communication Security]
[ ] Use end-to-end encrypted channels (e.g., Signal, encrypted Matrix) for all
    internal team coordination — no plaintext email or consumer messaging apps.
[ ] Establish a dedicated, engagement-specific communication workspace that is
    destroyed or archived securely upon engagement close.

[Category 2 - Infrastructure Setup]
[ ] Provision all C2 domains and cloud infrastructure using dedicated engagement
    accounts with no links to operator personal or company identity.
[ ] Deploy at least one redirector layer between the target environment and the
    actual C2 listener to prevent direct infrastructure attribution.
[ ] Verify that all C2 domain registration WHOIS records are privacy-protected
    and that hosting provider abuse contacts cannot trivially identify the operator.

[Category 3 - Tool Preparation]
[ ] Recompile all payloads and implants from source for each engagement;
    never deploy precompiled or publicly available binaries without modification.
[ ] Strip or spoof all metadata from payloads and documents (author name,
    company, compile path, PE timestamps) before staging.
[ ] Validate all tools against current AV and EDR signatures in an isolated
    lab environment before use; document evasion baseline per tool.

[Category 4 - Documentation Hygiene]
[ ] Store all client-related data, notes, and findings exclusively on
    encrypted, engagement-dedicated devices or secure vaults — never on
    personal laptops, phones, or unencrypted cloud storage.
[ ] Sanitize all internal planning documents to remove operator names,
    personal handles, and company-internal references before sharing
    any materials with the client.

Part C - Why Red Team OpSec Protects the Client:

Red team operational security is a professional and ethical obligation to the
client, not merely a self-protective habit. When red team infrastructure,
tools, or identities are exposed mid-engagement, the exercise results are
invalidated: the Blue Team has observed red team methods rather than genuine
attacker behavior, and the client receives a distorted picture of their true
security posture — potentially leading them to believe they are better or worse
protected than they actually are.

Beyond result integrity, a compromised red team operation introduces real risk
to the client's environment. Exposed C2 infrastructure can be taken over or
mimicked by actual threat actors who monitor red team activity, turning a
controlled exercise into a genuine attack surface. Client data handled without
proper hygiene — stored on personal devices, transmitted over unencrypted
channels, or logged in unsecured notes — represents a direct breach of the
trust and confidentiality agreement the client has extended to the red team.

Ultimately, a red team that cannot protect its own operational security cannot
credibly evaluate anyone else's. Maintaining strict OpSec demonstrates the
professionalism the client is paying for, preserves the legal and reputational
integrity of the engagement, and ensures that the findings delivered at the
end of the exercise reflect reality — not an operation that was burned before
it had a chance to test what it was designed to test.
